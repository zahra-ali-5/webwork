1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites

Copyright and other intellectual property laws affect websites because it may limit your ability to produce. Copyright is actually the right of someone who creates an original content. Only the owner has the right to reproduce the work, display the work in public and copy the work or sell copies of the work to other people. The only exception to copyright is if you use an image from public domains such as Google Images. It is a difficult area because not everyone has given their permission for their content to be reproduced. Fair Use is when you use content for educational or news reporting purposes or if you have transformed the content or only used part of it. The best way is to seek permission from the owner of the text/image.

1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using web pages and how we can overcome them

Lot of disabilities and conditions can affect the website accessibility, most common categories of impairments:

  • Visual Impairment: This includes a partial or total inability to see or to perceive color contrasts.
  • Hearing Impairment: Some users have a reduced ability to hear.
  • Motor Skills/Physical Disabilities: Users may have difficulty moving parts of their bodies, including making precise movements (such as when using a mouse).
  • Photosensitive Seizures: Conditions such as epilepsy can cause seizures that are often triggered by flashing lights.
  • Cognitive Disabilities: There are also many conditions that affect cognitive ability, such as dementia and dyslexia.

To work around these issues, many people use assistive technologies to browse the internet. This includes screen readers that vocalize the text on each page, speech recognition software that converts speech into text, Braille terminals, and even alternative keyboards that accommodate special needs.
